How to use shape symbols

Click any shape to copy. Use ● and ○ as solid and outline bullet points in checklists, completed/incomplete markers, or radio-button mockups. Diamonds ◆ ◇ make stylish bullets for headings. Triangle pointers (▶ ▼ ◀ ▲) work as expand/collapse indicators in documentation, play/pause hints, or directional cues. Half-filled circles (◐ ◑ ◒ ◓) are useful for progress indicators in plain-text status reports. Squares (■ □ ▢) are common in task lists, similar to checkbox UI. Hexagons (⬡ ⬢) are a 2020s aesthetic favorite for modern app branding and crypto/tech labels.

Where shape symbols shine

Custom bullet points

Replace boring • bullets with ◆ ▸ ▪ ◉ in lists and outlines for instant visual upgrade. Works in any text doc, slide deck, or social caption.

Progress and completion markers

Use ● for done, ○ for todo, ◐ for in-progress in plain-text checklists. Quick visual scan replaces the need for [x] / [ ] markdown.

Diagrams and flowcharts in text

Outline ASCII flowcharts with shapes for nodes and arrows for connections — ◆ decision, ◯ start, ■ process. Useful in code comments and design notes.

Stylized bio headers

Game and app UI mockups

Frequently asked questions

Why are there so many similar-looking circles?
Each circle variant covers a specific use case in Unicode. ○ (White Circle) and ● (Black Circle) are general-purpose. ◯ (Large Circle) is bigger. ◌ (Dotted Circle) marks placeholders in linguistic notation. ⊙ ⊕ ⊗ are mathematical operators. ◉ (Fisheye) is used in audio interfaces. The variation lets you pick exactly the right weight and meaning for your context.
Are hexagons widely supported?
⬡ (Light Hexagon) and ⬢ (Black Hexagon) were added in Unicode 5.1 (2008) and are supported in all modern fonts. Older Windows XP-era fonts may render them as missing glyphs, but on any device from the last decade they look correct.
Can I use shapes in a Twitter or Instagram name?
Yes, most shape symbols are accepted in display names on Twitter/X, Instagram, TikTok, and Discord. They sometimes get stripped from URL-style handles (the @username) but display names support nearly any Unicode character. Test by saving the profile and viewing it from a different account to be sure.
